    Combinatory calculus provides a limited set of primitive functions out of which other functions may be built.

    Combinatory Logic, invented by Shoenfinkel and developed by Curry and others in the 1920s is equivalent in expressive power to lambda calculus. The language contains three special symbols ("S", "K", and "I"), along with parentheses and variables (restricted here to single characters, e.g., "x" or "y").

    By convention, combination is left-associative, so (((SK)K)(yS)) can be written equivalently as SKK(yS). The meaning of "K" can be thought of as (λ x (λ y x)), the meaning of "S" can be thought of as (λ x (λ y (λ z ((x z)(y z))))). The other special symbol, "I", can be thought of as (λ x x), but can also be defined as SSK. Since this derivation works for any expression X, SKK is the identity function.

    BCKW

    A variant of combinatory logic that takes as primitive the combinators B, C, K, and W.

    Bx y zx (y z)Composition of x and y applied to z
    Cx y zx z ySwaps the second and third arguments
    Kx yxDiscards the second argument
    Wx yx y yDuplicates the second argument

    We always start from the left, applying the rule for the left-most combinator first. For example, WKxyz becomes Kxxyz, which becomes xyz.
